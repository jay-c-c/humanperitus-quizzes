Question 9
“Mature Industries tend to go out of own country and into low-cost assembly locations.” Which of the following theory/model explain this? (UGC NET Management Dec 2014)
  • A.Adam Smith’s theory of absolute advantage
  • B.Porter’s diamond model
  • C.Raymond Vermon’s international product lifecycle theory
  • D.Heckscher-Ohlin theory of international trade
Correct Answer: Raymond Vermon’s international product lifecycle theory

The statement "Mature industries tend to go out of their own country and into low-cost assembly locations" is closely related to the theory known as Raymond Vernon's International Product Lifecycle Theory. This theory, originally developed to describe the evolution of marketing strategies as a product matures, was adapted by Raymond Vernon to create a theory of international trade based on the life cycle of a product.

 

According to Vernon's theory, products go through three distinct stages in their life cycle:

 

1. New Product Stage: This is the initial stage where a firm develops and introduces an innovative product. During this stage, there is uncertainty about whether a profitable market for the product exists.

 

2. Maturing Product Stage: In this stage, demand for the product starts to grow rapidly as consumers recognize its value. To meet the increasing demand, the firm expands its production capacity by building new factories. This stage sees both domestic and foreign demand for the product increase.

 

3. Standardized Product Stage: As the product becomes more established and the market stabilizes, it starts to resemble a commodity. Firms producing the product face pressure to reduce manufacturing costs. To achieve this, they often shift production to countries with low labor costs. At this point, the product may also start to be imported back into the innovating firm's home market.

 

The key idea here is that in the standardized product stage, when the product has matured and become more of a commodity, firms seek to lower production costs by moving manufacturing to countries with cheaper labor. This corresponds to the statement in the question about mature industries relocating to low-cost assembly locations.

Question 11
Which one of the following International Trade theories, the ‘Leontief paradox’ pertains to (UGC NET Management Jun 2020)
  • A.Comparative Advantage theory
  • B.Product life cycle theory
  • C.Heckscher-Ohlin Theory
  • D.Absolute advantage theory
Correct Answer: Heckscher-Ohlin Theory

The Heckscher-Ohlin theory suggests a country should export those goods that intensively use those factors of production that are relatively abundant in the country. The theory was tested empirically by economist Wassily Leontief using input-output analysis, a mathematical technique for measuring the interrelationships among the sectors of an economy. Leontief used his input-output model of the economy to estimate the quantities of labor and capital needed to produce ‘bundles’ of U.S. exports and imports. These results were not consistent with the predictions of the Heckscher-Ohlin theory. The results were that a country with a higher capital per worker has a lower capital/labor ratio in exports than in imports i.e., the imports of a capital-intensive country were more capital intensive than its exports. This econometric finding resulted in Leontief’s Paradox.

Question 12
Theory of Comparative Advantage given by David Ricardo in 1817 is (UGC NET Management Dec 2019)
  • A.Two Commodity and One Factor Theory
  • B.Two Commodity and Two Factor Theory
  • C.Two Country, Two Commodity and Two Factor Theory
  • D.Two Country, Two Commodity and One Factor Theory
Correct Answer: Two Country, Two Commodity and One Factor Theory

David Ricardo's Theory of Comparative Advantage, developed in 1817, is a fundamental concept in international trade. It is often summarized as the idea that a country should specialize in and export goods in which it has a comparative advantage while importing goods in which it has a comparative disadvantage. Here's an explanation of the theory and its key assumptions based on the provided information:

 

Theory of Comparative Advantage: David Ricardo's theory of comparative advantage explains how countries can benefit from international trade by specializing in the production of goods where they have a comparative advantage, i.e., a lower opportunity cost. This theory suggests that even if a country is less efficient in producing all goods compared to another country, there are still gains from trade when they exchange the goods in which they have a relative efficiency or comparative advantage.

 

Key Assumptions of the Theory:

1. Two Country, Two Commodity, and One Factor: Ricardo's model simplifies international trade analysis by considering two countries, two commodities, and one factor of production. This simplification allows for a straightforward illustration of the concept.

 

2. Labour Theory of Value: The theory assumes the labor theory of value, which means that the value of a commodity is determined by the amount of labor required to produce it. In this model, production costs are measured primarily in terms of labor costs.

 

3. Constant Returns to Scale: The production function is assumed to exhibit constant returns to scale. This means that output per unit of labor remains constant over relevant ranges of production. It simplifies the analysis but is a simplification of real-world production functions.

 

4. Immobility of Factors Internationally: While factors of production (like labor) can move within countries, they are considered completely immobile internationally. This assumption emphasizes the differences in factor endowments between countries.

 

5. Zero Transport Costs: The model assumes that there are no costs associated with transporting goods between countries, making it a frictionless world for trade.

 

6. Free Trade Policy: The model operates under the assumption of free trade, where there are no tariffs, quotas, or other trade barriers.

The four causes of disequilibrium in the balance of payments:

 

Cause 1 - Cyclical Disequilibrium

Cyclical disequilibrium occurs due to fluctuations in economic activity, known as trade cycles. During periods of prosperity, prices may fall, and people's incomes may decrease, affecting exports and imports. This fluctuation in income and prices can influence the balance of payments. For instance, if prices rise during prosperity, countries with a high sensitivity to import prices may experience a decrease in imports. Secular disequilibrium arises from long-term and deep-seated changes in an economy as it progresses through different stages of development. In the initial stages of development, domestic savings may fall short of domestic investment, leading to an import surplus that is challenging to finance. Later, when domestic savings exceed domestic investment, exports may surpass imports. Disequilibrium can occur when long-term capital movements do not align with these changes.   • D.K Curve Theory
Correct Answer: J Curve Theory

J Curve Theory is an economic concept that elucidates the short-term impact of a currency devaluation or depreciation on a nation's balance of trade. The nomenclature of this theory, the "J Curve," is attributed to the characteristic pattern it exhibits in graphical representation. It entails a temporary deterioration in the trade balance, depicted as a downward sloping "J," before transitioning to an eventual improvement in the long run.

 

When a country opts for currency devaluation, its exports become more competitively priced for foreign buyers, while concurrently, imports become relatively more expensive for domestic consumers. In the immediate aftermath, the surge in import costs may outweigh the increase in export competitiveness, leading to a temporary worsening of the trade balance.

 

However, as time progresses, the effects of this currency devaluation begin to manifest. Export volumes tend to respond positively to the now-attractive prices, while import demand adjusts to the escalated costs. This gradual adaptation leads to an eventual enhancement of the trade balance. This amelioration is often visualized as the ascending curve of the "J," signifying that, in due course, the trade balance rebounds to a more favorable position, potentially even attaining a surplus.

 

In summation, the J Curve Theory elucidates the transitory deterioration and the subsequent amelioration of a nation's balance of trade in reaction to currency devaluation.

An Export Oriented Unit (EOU) is an industrial unit or manufacturing facility in India that is set up with the primary objective of producing goods for export to international markets. EOUs are part of a government initiative to promote exports and boost economic growth by facilitating the production of goods that are globally competitive. Here are key points to understand about EOUs:

 

The fundamental characteristic of an EOU is its exclusive focus on exports. These units are required to export the entire production they generate. This means that the goods produced within an EOU cannot be sold in the domestic market but must be shipped abroad.

 

Unlike Export Processing Zones (EPZs) or Free Trade Zones (FTZs), which have fixed geographical boundaries, EOUs are not restricted to specific areas. They can be established in various locations across the country, including near the source of raw materials or specialized labor.

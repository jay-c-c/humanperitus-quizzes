Question 13

  • D.Operating lease
Correct Answer: Leveraged lease

Leveraged Lease: A leveraged lease arrangement involves the lessor securing partial financing for the lease from a third-party financial institution. Under this structure, the lending company holds ownership of the leased asset, while the lessor engages in a lease agreement with the lessee and manages the collection of payments. These payments are then directed to the lender, completing the financial arrangement.

 

Financial Lease: A finance lease, also referred to as a capital lease or sales lease, is a leasing arrangement wherein a finance company typically assumes legal ownership of the asset throughout the lease term. However, the lessee retains operational control over the asset and also bears a portion of the economic risks and benefits associated with changes in the asset's valuation.

This arrangement is made immediately following the sale, with details such as lease payments and duration established at the outset. Essentially, the seller transitions into the lessee role, while the purchaser assumes the lessor position.

 

Operating Lease: An operating lease is a contractual arrangement that grants the lessee the right to utilize an asset without transferring ownership. Unlike a finance lease, an operating lease does not confer ownership rights to the lessee. Instead, it represents a form of off-balance sheet financing wherein the leased asset and associated future rent liabilities are not recorded on the lessee's balance sheet.

Which of the following are referred to as the ‘Term structure theories’?1. Preferred Habitat Theory2. Unbiased Expectation Theory3. Immunization TheoryCodes:
  • A.1 and 2
  • B.2 and 3
  • C.1 and 3
  • D.1, 2 and 3
Correct Answer: 1 and 2

The term structure theories are frameworks that attempt to explain the relationship between interest rates or bond yields and different terms or maturities. Here's an explanation of the three main term structure theories:

 

1. Unbiased Expectation Theory: This theory suggests that short-term interest rates can be forecasted using long-term interest rates. It posits that investors form expectations about future interest rates based on current information and economic conditions. According to this theory, investors can roll over short-term investments to match the maturity of longer-term investments, effectively forecasting future short-term rates. In other words, the yield curve reflects market expectations about future interest rate movements.

 

2. Liquidity Preference Theory: The Liquidity Preference Theory proposes that forward rates, which represent future spot rates, include a liquidity premium. Investors demand compensation for holding longer-term bonds due to the increased risk associated with fluctuations in interest rates over time. Therefore, forward rates are composed of expected future spot rates plus a liquidity premium. This theory suggests that investors prefer shorter maturities because they offer greater liquidity and less exposure to interest rate risk.

 

3. Preferred Habitat Theory: Unlike the Unbiased Expectation Theory and Liquidity Preference Theory, which focus on interest rate expectations and liquidity premiums, the Preferred Habitat Theory emphasizes the relationship between supply and demand for funds at different maturities. According to this theory, investors have preferences or "habitats" for certain maturities based on factors such as investment horizon, risk tolerance, and market conditions. Changes in the yield curve shape can be explained by shifts in these preferences, as investors adjust their holdings to match their preferred habitats.

Question 15
Which one of the following is considered a necessary precondition by Richard Roll for markets to become efficient? (UGC NET Commerce Jun 2021)
  • A.Traders who make a living by 'beating the market' through trading
  • B.Free access to and flow of information
  • C.Costless trading
  • D.Prices approximate a random walk
Correct Answer: Traders who make a living by 'beating the market' through trading

Richard Roll's perspective on market efficiency is multifaceted, emphasizing several key principles that are fundamental to understanding how markets operate. One of the necessary preconditions identified by Roll for markets to become efficient is the presence of traders who actively seek to "beat the market" through trading activities.

 

Let's delve into Roll's insights in more detail:

 

Efficient Market Hypothesis (EMH)

Roll's views on market efficiency align with the Efficient Market Hypothesis (EMH), which posits that in an efficient market, asset prices fully reflect all available information. This means that it is difficult for investors to consistently outperform the market by exploiting information asymmetries.

 

No Free Lunch Principle

Roll applies the "no free lunch" principle to efficient financial markets, suggesting that costless trading strategies will not generate excess returns. Even if traders engage in active trading to exploit perceived mispricings, the returns from such activities will, on average, be no larger than those achieved by randomly selecting a portfolio.

 

Role of Professional Traders

Roll emphasizes the importance of professional traders who actively participate in the market to uncover and exploit mispricings. These traders, often equipped with sophisticated tools and resources, aim to capitalize on market inefficiencies to generate profits. Without the presence of such traders, prices may fail to fully reflect all available information. Thus Option A is correct.

 

Information Cost and Accessibility

Roll acknowledges that relevant information necessary for pricing assets is often difficult and expensive to uncover and evaluate. Thus, while market prices may not reflect all information, the connection between unreflected information and prices may be subtle and challenging to detect without significant resources.

 

Random Walk Theory

Efficient market prices should approximate a random walk, implying that they fluctuate more or less randomly over time. Deviations from randomness may occur, but they are typically costly to discern and may not offer consistent opportunities for profit.

Which form of market efficiency states that current security prices fully reflect all information, both public and private?

Correct Answer: Strong.

Eugene Fama, a prominent economist, proposed three levels of market efficiency:

 

1. Weak-form efficiency: In this form, current prices reflect all past trading information, such as past prices and trading volumes. Essentially, this means that past price movements cannot be used to predict future price movements.

 

2. Semi-strong-form efficiency: In this form, current prices reflect all publicly available information, including company financial statements, news, and other widely disseminated information. Investors cannot consistently achieve abnormal returns by trading on public information alone, as the market quickly incorporates this information into stock prices.

 

3. Strong-form efficiency: This is the highest level of efficiency, where current prices reflect all information, both public and private. This includes not only publicly available information but also information known only to insiders, such as corporate executives. In a strongly efficient market, even insiders cannot consistently earn abnormal returns by trading on their private information because it is already reflected in the stock prices.

  • B.Retained earnings

  • D.Preference share capital
Correct Answer: Retained earnings

The correct answer is (B) Retained earnings.

Retained earnings represent the portion of net income that a company retains rather than distributing it as dividends to shareholders. While retained earnings do not involve explicit costs such as interest payments (as in the case of debt) or dividends (as in the case of equity), they still have an implicit cost of capital associated with them.

 

The implicit cost of retained earnings arises from the opportunity cost of not distributing dividends to shareholders. When a company retains earnings, shareholders forego the opportunity to invest those earnings elsewhere to earn a return. Therefore, the implicit cost of retained earnings is the return that shareholders could have earned if the earnings had been distributed as dividends or used for other investments. This implicit cost is reflected in the opportunity cost of capital for shareholders.

Question 34
While calculating the weighted average cost of capital, the weights can be assigned in which of the ways:
  • A.Historical or existing weights
  • B.Book Value Weights
  • C.Market Value Weights
  • D.All the above
Correct Answer: All the above

In the calculation of weighted average cost of capital, the Weights can be given in the following ways:

 

1. Historical or existing weights: Historical or existing weights are the weights based on the actual or existing proportions of different sources in the overall capital structure. Such weighing system is based on the actual proportions at the time when the WACC is being calculated. In other words, the weighing system is the proportions in which the funds have already been raised by the firm.

 

2. Book Value Weights: The weights are said to be book value weights if the proportions of different sources are ascertained on the basis of the face values i.e., the accounting values. The book value weights can be easily calculated by taking the relevant information from the capital structure as given in the balance sheet of the firm. The book value weights are considered as a sound weighing system as it is operational in nature and a firm may design its capital structure in terms of as it appears in the balance sheet. However, the book value weights system does not truly reflect the economic values. In fact, the weighing system should be market determined. The book value weights system is not consistent with the definition of the overall cost of capital, which is defined as the minimum rate of return needed to maintain the firm’s market value. The book value weights ignore the market values.

 

3. Market Value Weights: The weights may also be calculated on the basis of the market value of different sources i.e., the proportion of each source at its market value. In order to calculate the market value weights, the firm has to find out the current market price of the securities in each category. However, a problem may arise if there is no market value available for a particular type of security.

 

4. Marginal Weights: The other system of assigning weights is the marginal weights system. The marginal weights refer to the proportions in which the firm wants or intends to raise funds from different sources. In other words, the proportions in which additional funds required to finance the investment proposals will be raised are known as marginal weights. So, in case of marginal weights, the firm in fact, calculates the actual WACC of the incremental funds. Theoretically, the system of marginal weights seems to be good enough as the return from investment will be compared with the actual cost of funds.

The pecking order theory, proposed by Myers and Majluf in 1984, suggests that firms have a hierarchy of preferred sources of financing. This hierarchy is based on the principle of minimizing information asymmetry and transaction costs. In the pecking order theory, managers prefer to finance the firm's capital structure in the following order:

 

a. Retained earnings and internal sources: Retained earnings are the preferred source of financing because they do not involve issuing new securities or incurring transaction costs. Internal sources such as cash reserves and profits are utilized first to fund investment opportunities.

b. External debt financing: When internal funds are insufficient, firms prefer to raise capital through external debt financing, such as bank loans or bonds. Debt financing is favored over equity financing because it does not dilute ownership and does not require disclosing sensitive information to external investors.

c. Hybrid security and convertible bonds: If debt financing is not feasible or sufficient, firms may consider issuing hybrid securities or convertible bonds. These securities combine characteristics of both debt and equity and may be attractive to investors seeking a mix of fixed income and potential equity upside.

d. New issue of equity share: Issuing new equity shares is considered the last resort for financing under the pecking order theory. Equity financing involves higher information asymmetry and agency costs compared to debt financing. Therefore, firms prefer to avoid issuing new equity shares unless all other financing options are exhausted.

Therefore, the correct order based on the pecking order theory is: a, b, c and d, which corresponds to option (B).

  • D.II, I, III
Correct Answer: I, III, II

The pecking order theory suggests a hierarchy of financing choices for companies, prioritizing internal financing over external sources. According to this theory, the preferred order of financing choices is as follows:

 

1. Reinvestment of earnings: The first preference is to utilize internally generated funds, such as retained earnings, for financing purposes. Reinvesting earnings allows companies to maintain control and avoid the costs associated with external financing.

2. Issue of debt: If internal funds are insufficient, the next preference is to raise capital through debt instruments. Debt financing involves borrowing funds from creditors, such as banks or bondholders, and typically offers tax advantages and fixed interest payments.

3. New issue of equity: As a last resort, companies may resort to issuing new equity to raise capital. Equity financing involves selling ownership stakes in the company to investors in exchange for capital. However, issuing new equity can dilute existing shareholders' ownership and may signal undervaluation to the market.

Question 26
Pecking order theory in finance is based on the assertion of (UGC NET Commerce Jun 2021)
  • A.Asymmetric information between managers and investors
  • B.Symmetric information between managers and investors
  • C.Outside information
  • D.Asymmetric information among investors only
Correct Answer: Asymmetric information between managers and investors

The pecking order theory of capital structure, formalized by Myers (1984) and Myers and Majluf (1984), posits that firms have a preference ranking over financing sources because of asymmetric information between managers and investors. Asymmetric information, also known as information failure, occurs when one party possesses more (better) information than another party, which causes an imbalance in transaction power.

 

Company managers typically possess more information regarding the company’s performance, prospects, risks, and future outlook than external users such as creditors (debt holders) and investors (shareholders). Therefore, to compensate for information asymmetry, external users demand a higher return to counter the risk that they are taking. In essence, due to information asymmetry, external sources of finances demand a higher rate of return to compensate for higher risk.

 

In the context of the pecking order theory, retained earnings financing (internal financing) comes directly from the company and minimizes information asymmetry. As opposed to external financing, such as debt or equity financing where the company must incur fees to obtain external financing, internal financing is the cheapest and most convenient source of financing.

When a company finances an investment opportunity through external financing (debt or equity), a higher return is demanded because creditors and investors possess less information regarding the company, as opposed to managers. In terms of external financing, managers prefer to use debt over equity – the cost of debt is lower compared to the cost of equity.

 

The issuance of debt often signals an undervalued stock and confidence that the board believes the investment is profitable. On the other hand, the issuance of equity sends a negative signal that the stock is overvalued and that the management is looking to generate financing by diluting shares in the company.

When thinking of the pecking order theory, it is useful to consider the seniority of claims to assets. Debtholders require a lower return as opposed to stockholders because they are entitled to a higher claim to assets (in the event of a bankruptcy). Therefore, when considering sources of financing, the cheapest is through retained earnings, second through debt, and third through equity.

Question 16
According to the traditional approach, what is the effect of increase in degree of leverage on the valuation of a firm? (UGC NET Commerce Dec 2022)
  • A.Increases
  • B.Decreases
  • C.Remains unaffected
  • D.Increases first and then decreases
Correct Answer: Increases first and then decreases

The traditional approach to capital structure assumes that there is an optimal mix of debt and equity that maximizes a firm's overall value. This optimal mix is determined by finding the level of debt that balances the tax benefits of debt with the costs of financial distress and bankruptcy.

 

When a firm increases its degree of leverage (i.e., the proportion of debt in its capital structure), several effects are observed. Initially, as the degree of leverage increases, the cost of capital decreases due to the tax benefits of debt. This results in an increase in the firm's value.

 

However, as the degree of leverage increases further, the costs of financial distress and bankruptcy become more significant, leading to an increase in the cost of capital. This, in turn, reduces the firm's overall value. In addition, high levels of debt can also lead to agency costs and conflicts of interest between shareholders and creditors, which can further reduce the firm's value.

 

Therefore, according to the traditional approach, the effect of an increase in the degree of leverage on the valuation of a firm is that it increases initially due to the tax benefits of debt, but then decreases as the costs of financial distress, bankruptcy, and other factors outweigh the benefits of debt. The optimal level of debt that maximizes the firm's overall value is the point at which the tax benefits of debt are balanced with the costs of financial distress and bankruptcy, and this point varies depending on the specific characteristics of the firm, its industry, and the broader economic environment.

Organochlorine pesticides, such as DDT, have a lower direct human toxicity but can still pose a risk to humans through their impact on the food chain. The properties that make them impactful on the food chain are:

 

1. Persistence: Organochlorine pesticides are highly persistent in the environment, meaning they do not easily break down. As a result, they can accumulate in the bodies of organisms over time.

This can lead to higher levels of these pesticides in organisms at the top of the food chain, including humans, which can result in acute human toxicity when consumed.

  • D.(a)-(i), (b)-(ii), (c)-(iv), (d)-(iii)
Correct Answer: (a)-(iv), (b)-(i), (c)-(ii), (d)-(iii)

Histosols (from the Greek histos – tissue) are mainly composed of organic material in their upper portion. The Histosol order mostly contains soils commonly called bogs, moors, peatlands, muskegs, fens, or peats and mucks. These soils form when organic matter, such as leaves, mosses, or grasses, decomposes more slowly than it accumulates due to a decrease in microbial decay rates. This most often occurs in extremely wet areas or underwater; thus, most of these soils are saturated year-round.

 

Andisols (from the Japanese ando – black soil) typically form from the weathering of volcanic materials such as ash, resulting in minerals in the soil with poor crystal structure. These minerals have an unusually high capacity to hold both nutrients and water, making these soils very productive and fertile. Andisols include weakly weathered soils with much volcanic glass, as well as more strongly weathered soils.

 

Gelisols (from the Latin gelare – to freeze) are soils that are permanently frozen (contain “permafrost”) or contain evidence of permafrost near the soil surface. Gelisols are found in the Arctic and Antarctic, as well as at extremely high elevations.

 

Aridisols (from the Latin aridus – dry) are soils that occur in climates that are too dry for “mesophytic” plants—plants adapted to neither a too wet nor too dry environments—to survive. The climate in which Aridisols occur also restricts soil weathering processes. Aridisols often contain accumulations of salt, gypsum, or carbonates, and are found in hot and cold deserts worldwide.

Soil particles are classified based on their size. The smallest particles are clay, followed by silt, then sand, and finally gravel which has the largest particles. Thus, the correct ascending order of soil particles by size is Clay < Silt < Sand < Gravel.

Compost is decomposed organic material. Compost is made with material such as leaves, shredded twigs, and kitchen scraps from plants.

To gardeners, compost is considered "black gold" because of its many benefits in the garden. Compost is a great material for garden soil. Adding compost to clay soils makes them easier to work and plant. In sandy soils, the addition of compost improves the water holding capacity of the soil. By adding organic matter to the soil, compost can help improve plant growth and health.

Composting is also a good way to recycle leaves and other yard waste. Instead of paying a company to haul away leaves, you can compost the leaves and return the nutrients to your garden.

Vermicompost- The compost is ready in 60 to 90 days and the material becomes moderately loose, crumbly with dark brown colour. It will be black, granular, lightweight and humus-rich. The pits are filled layer by layer, each layer being 15 cm thick. The second turning is done after another month and the third two weeks later. The material is allowed to decompose for 3-6 months and produces about 8 tonnes of compost per pit.

Dobson units are typically used in the context of measuring ozone depletion and monitoring the ozone layer.

 

Parts per million (ppm) is a unit of concentration that expresses the proportion of ozone molecules in a given volume of air. It represents the number of ozone molecules per one million molecules of air.

The Kyoto Protocol is an international treaty that was adopted in 1997 as an extension of the United Nations Framework Convention on Climate Change (UNFCCC). It aimed to address the issue of global climate change by setting binding targets for the reduction of greenhouse gas (GHG) emissions. Some key features of the Kyoto Protocol:

 

1) Binding Emission Reduction Targets: The Kyoto Protocol established legally binding emission reduction targets for developed countries (referred to as Annex I countries). These countries were required to reduce their GHG emissions by specific percentages below their 1990 levels over a commitment period of 2008-2012.

 

2) Flexible Mechanisms: The Protocol introduced three market-based mechanisms to help countries meet their emission reduction targets more cost-effectively. These mechanisms are:

a. Clean Development Mechanism (CDM): Allows Annex I countries to invest in emission reduction projects in developing countries and receive credits for the emissions reduced, which can be counted towards their own targets.

b. Joint Implementation (JI): Enables Annex I countries to collaborate on emission reduction projects with other Annex I countries and receive credits for the emissions reduced.

c. Emissions Trading: Allows Annex I countries to trade their emission allowances with one another, providing flexibility in meeting their targets.

 

3) Compliance Mechanism: The Kyoto Protocol established a compliance mechanism to ensure that countries fulfill their commitments. It includes a compliance committee that reviews and assesses the compliance of countries with their emission reduction targets and addresses any non-compliance issues.

 

4) Adaptation Fund: The Protocol established an Adaptation Fund to assist developing countries in adapting to the adverse effects of climate change. The fund is financed by a share of proceeds from the CDM projects.

 

5) Reporting and Review: The Protocol requires countries to report their GHG emissions and removals and undergo regular reviews of their progress in implementing their commitments.

Question 39
Identify from the options given below, the co-benefit of Montreal Protocol (UGC NET 4th Dec 2019 Morning Paper)

  • B.Reduction in carbon dioxide (equivalent)emissions
  • C.Convergence of efforts of international community in addressing air pollution
  • D.Control of transboundary movement of hazardous waste
Correct Answer: Reduction in carbon dioxide (equivalent)emissions

The Montreal Protocol on Substances that Deplete the Ozone Layer is the landmark multilateral environmental agreement that regulates the production and consumption of nearly 100 man-made chemicals referred to as ozone depleting substances (ODS). When released to the atmosphere, those chemicals damage the stratospheric ozone layer, Earth’s protective shield that protects humans and the environment from harmful levels of ultraviolet radiation from the sun. Adopted on 15 September 1987, the Protocol is to date the only UN treaty ever that has been ratified every country on Earth - all 197 UN Member States.

On January 1, 2019 the Kigali Amendment to the Montreal Protocol came into force. Under the Kigali Amendment countries promised to reduce the use of hydrofluorocarbons (HFCs) by more than 80% over the next 30 years. By December 27, 2018, 65 countries had ratified the Amendment.

 

Produced mostly in developed countries, hydrofluorocarbons (HFCs) replaced CFCs and HCFCs. HFCs pose no harm to the ozone layer because, unlike CFCs and HCFCs, they do not contain chlorine. They are, however, greenhouse gases, with a high global warming potential (GWP), comparable to that of CFCs and HCFCs. In 2009, a study calculated that a fast phasedown of high-GWP HFCs could potentially prevent the equivalent of up to 8.8 Gt CO2-eq per year in emissions by 2050. A proposed phasedown of HFCs was hence projected to avoid up to 0.5°C of warming by 2100 under the high-HFC growth scenario, and up to 0.35°C under the low-HFC growth scenario. Recognizing the opportunity presented for fast and effective phasing down of HFCs through the Montreal Protocol, starting in 2009 the Federated States of Micronesia proposed an amendment to phase down high-GWP HFCs, with the U.S., Canada, and Mexico following with a similar proposal in 2010. Therefore, the co-profit of Montreal protocol has been reduction in CO2 emission.

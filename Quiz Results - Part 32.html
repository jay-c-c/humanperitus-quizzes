Question 3
In valuation of consignment stock only _______ expenses are added.
  • A.Direct
  • B.Indirect
  • C.Both (a) and (b)
  • D.None of the above
Correct Answer: Direct

In valuation of consignment stock, only direct expenses are added. In the case of consignment, cost means not only the cost of the goods as such to the consignor but also all expenses incurred till the goods reach the premises of the consignee. These are called direct expenses. Such expenses include packaging, freight, cartage, insurance in transit, octroi, import duty etc. But expenses incurred after the goods have reached the consignee’s godown (such as godown rent, insurance of godown, delivery charges, salesman salaries) are not treated as part of the value of consignment stock. These are called indirect expenses.

  • A.Normal loss
  • B.Abnormal loss
  • C.Loss of bad debts
  • D.Loss by riots
Correct Answer: Normal loss

Normal loss is an unavoidable loss due to some inherent features of the product. It is treated as a part of cost by inflating cost per unit. No separate Journal entry is passed for normal loss. Normal loss impacts the gross profit.

Whereas abnormal loss is a loss which is accidental in nature. For example, loss due to an earthquake. The total cost of abnormal cost (whether recovered or not) is credited to Consignment account. Abnormal loss is valued just like inventories/stock in hand. Abnormal loss does not impact gross profit.

Agency theory is concerned with the branch of economics relating the behavior of principals and their agents. The relationship between stockholders and management is called an agency relationship. Such a relationship exists whenever someone (the principal) hires another (the agent) to represent his or her interests. In all such relationships there is a possibility of a conflict of interest between the principal and the agent. Such a conflict is called an agency problem.

 

Agency theory is a cornerstone concept in financial management, addressing the complex relationships and potential conflicts of interest that arise between various stakeholders within an organization. At its core, agency theory revolves around the principal-agent relationship, where the principal (such as shareholders) delegates decision-making authority to the agent (such as managers or executives), with the expectation that the agent will act in the best interests of the principal. However, due to divergent interests and information asymmetry, conflicts may arise, leading to agency costs and inefficiencies.

 

One of the key roles of agency theory in financial management is to align the interests of managers with those of shareholders. This alignment is crucial for ensuring that managers prioritize actions that maximize shareholder wealth and organizational value. To achieve this alignment, financial management practices often incorporate incentive mechanisms, such as executive compensation packages tied to performance metrics like stock price or earnings per share. These incentives aim to motivate managers to make decisions that are consistent with the long-term interests of shareholders.

 

Moreover, agency theory informs the design of monitoring and control mechanisms to ensure that managers act in accordance with the objectives of shareholders. Effective monitoring is essential for reducing the agency costs associated with opportunistic behavior, moral hazard, and adverse selection. Financial management practices often include regular financial reporting, internal controls, and external audits to provide transparency and accountability, enabling shareholders to assess managerial performance and make informed decisions.

 

Another critical role of agency theory in financial management is in optimizing the capital structure of a firm. Agency theory recognizes the trade-offs between debt and equity financing and the implications for the risk-return profile of the organization. It suggests that excessive debt may lead to conflicts between shareholders and bondholders, known as the agency cost of debt. Financial managers must balance these competing interests to determine the optimal mix of debt and equity that minimizes agency costs while maximizing firm value.

 

Furthermore, agency theory informs corporate governance practices aimed at enhancing the oversight and accountability of managers to shareholders. Corporate governance mechanisms, such as the composition of the board of directors, the role of independent directors, and the implementation of governance structures like board committees and shareholder rights, are designed to mitigate agency conflicts and promote ethical behavior and stewardship of shareholder resources.

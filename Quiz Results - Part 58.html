In microeconomics, the correct sequence in respect to the internal issues of business firms is (A) What to produce, how to produce, how much to produce, how to determine the price of the product.

 

(i) What to produce?

Firms first need to decide what products or commodities they are going to produce. They choose the products based on market demand and profitability.

 

(ii) How to produce?

Once the product is determined, firms must decide how to produce it efficiently. This involves choosing the appropriate technology, production methods, and resource allocation to minimize costs.

 

(iii) How much to produce?

After deciding what and how to produce, firms need to determine the quantity they will produce. This quantity is typically set based on market demand and cost considerations. Firms aim to produce the quantity that maximizes their profit.

 

(iv) How to determine the price of the product?

Finally, firms need to determine the price at which they will sell their product. Pricing decisions are crucial for profitability and involve factors such as production costs, competition, and consumer demand.

 

Therefore, option (A) is the correct sequence of internal issues that business firms address in microeconomics.

Question 23
Which kind of economics explains the phenomenon of cause-and-effect relationship?(UGC NET Commerce Dec 2012)
  • A.Normative
  • B.Positive
  • C.Micro
  • D.Macro
Correct Answer: Positive

The branch of economics that explains the phenomenon of cause-and-effect relationships is positive economics.

 

Normative economics primarily deals with idealistic situations and expresses what should be in an ideal world, often based on subjective judgments and opinions.

 

Positive economics, on the other hand, deals with real-world situations and describes the world as it is. It focuses on understanding cause-and-effect relationships and can be tested, amended, or rejected based on empirical evidence. Positive economics relies on objective judgments and data.

 

In addition to this distinction, economics can be categorized into microeconomics and macroeconomics:

 

Microeconomics examines economic issues at the individual or small-scale level, providing insights into individual prices, consumption, and production. It particularly emphasizes price analysis.

 

Macroeconomics, on the other hand, studies economic problems at the level of the entire economy. It deals with concepts like national income, total output, total consumption, and the general price level, focusing on income analysis and the overall performance of the economy.

  • A.Substitution Effect
  • B.Income Effect
  • C.Utility-Maximizing Behaviour
  • D.Inflation Effect
Correct Answer: Inflation Effect

The Law of Demand states that, all else being equal, as the price of a commodity decreases, the quantity demanded for that commodity increases, and conversely, as the price of the commodity increases, the quantity demanded decreases. The Law of Demand is a fundamental principle in economics and is based on observable consumer behavior in the market.

 

The factors that drive the Law of Demand are as follows:

 

Substitution Effect: When the price of a commodity falls while the prices of its substitutes remain constant, the commodity becomes relatively cheaper. Consumers tend to substitute costlier goods with the now cheaper commodity, leading to an increase in its demand. This effect is known as the substitution effect.

 

Income Effect: A decrease in the price of a commodity, with other factors held constant, effectively increases the consumer's real income in terms of that commodity. With more purchasing power, consumers are inclined to buy more of the commodity. This increase in demand due to increased real income is termed the income effect. The income effect can be negative for inferior goods, where a price decrease leads consumers to buy less of the inferior good and more of a superior one.

 

Utility-Maximizing Behavior: Consumers aim to maximize their satisfaction or utility within their budget constraints. The Law of Demand is influenced by the consumer's rational choice to allocate their spending in a way that maximizes utility. When a commodity's price falls, it becomes more attractive in terms of utility, prompting consumers to increase their demand for it.

 

In essence, these factors collectively explain why consumers respond to price changes by adjusting their purchasing behavior. However, the Law of Demand is not affected by the inflation effect, as inflation is a general increase in the overall price level in the economy over time and does not directly impact the relationship between a specific commodity's price and its quantity demanded.

1. Identification of Variables: The first step is to identify the relevant variables that affect the demand for a particular product or service. These variables can include factors like the price of the product, consumer income, advertising expenditure, and other related variables. Identification is crucial because it defines the scope of the analysis and helps determine which factors are considered as independent variables (factors that influence demand) and which is the dependent variable (the quantity demanded).

 

2. Collection of Data: Once the variables are identified, the next step is to gather data related to these variables. This data collection process involves obtaining historical or real-time data on the variables of interest. For example, if price and quantity demanded are variables of interest, data on past prices and corresponding quantities sold over a period are collected. 3. Mathematical Specification of the Relationship Among the Variables: After collecting the data, economists and analysts need to specify the mathematical relationship between the variables. Econometric methods, including regression analysis, are often employed for parameter estimation.

Using the Estimates to Arrive at the Estimates of Variables: Once the parameters of the demand function are estimated, they can be used to make predictions or estimates about the dependent variable (quantity demanded) under different scenarios. This step allows businesses or policymakers to assess how changes in the independent variables (e.g., price changes, income fluctuations) will impact the quantity demanded. A formal definition of price elasticity of demand (ep) is given as:

ep= Percentage change in quantity demandedPercentage change in price

ep= ∆QQ÷∆PP= ∆Q∆P×PQ

where

Q = quantity demanded,

P= original price,

ΔQ change in quantity demanded and ΔP change in price.

It is important to note here that a minus sign (-) is generally inserted in the formula before the fraction in order to make the elasticity coefficient a non-negative value.

 

The goods can be arranged in the increasing order of price elasticities in the following order: Necessities, differentiated products, durable goods, homogeneous products.

 

Necessities are perfectly inelastic (ep=0) because irrespective of any rise or fall in price of a commodity, the quantity demanded remains the same. The elasticity of demand in this case will be equal to zero.

 

Differentiated products do not have substitutes. The availability of substitutes for a given product has a direct impact on its price elasticity. Differentiated products are relatively inelastic (ep<1)because given percentage change in price produces a relatively less percentage change in quantity demanded. In such a case, elasticity of demand is said to be less than one.

 

Durable goods have a number of substitutes. Therefore, a small change in price leads to a big change in quantity demanded. Thus, durable goods are relatively elastic (ep>1).

 

Homogeneous products have a large number of substitutes available in the market. Thus, a little change in price leads to an infinite change in quantity demanded. Therefore, homogeneous products are perfectly elastic (ep=∞).

Correct Answer: Shut down costs

Shut-down costs: These costs are incurred when a company temporarily suspends its production operations. It's essential to differentiate shut-down costs from other cost-related terms:

Question 33
Which law is behind the U shape of Long run average cost curve?
  • A.Law of variable proportions
  • B.Law of returns to scale
  • C.Law of Marginal utility
  • D.None of the above
Correct Answer: Law of returns to scale

The U shape of the Long Run Average Cost (LRAC) curve is primarily explained by the "Law of Returns to Scale," so the correct answer is (B) Law of returns to scale.

 

The Law of Returns to Scale examines how changes in the scale of production (i.e., the size of the firm) affect the long-run average cost of production. It describes how long-run average costs can change as a firm increases or decreases its scale of operations. When a firm initially increases its scale of production, it may experience economies of scale, leading to a decrease in average costs per unit of output, which causes the LRAC curve to slope downward. However, if a firm keeps increasing its scale beyond a certain point, it may encounter diseconomies of scale, causing average costs to rise, leading to the upward-sloping portion of the LRAC curve.

 

So, the U shape of the LRAC curve reflects the interplay between economies of scale and diseconomies of scale as a firm adjusts its size in the long run.

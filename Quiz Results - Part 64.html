The sign test is a statistical hypothesis test that is used when the data is not normally distributed or the sample size is small. It is a non-parametric test because it does not make any assumptions about the underlying distribution of the data.

 

The sign test is used to test whether the median of a sample is equal to a hypothesized value. It works by first calculating the difference between each observation in the sample and the hypothesized median. Then, the sign of each difference is recorded as either positive, negative, or zero. Finally, the number of positive and negative signs is compared to determine if there is a significant difference between the sample median and the hypothesized median.

 

The sign test is simple and easy to use, and it is often used in situations where the data does not meet the assumptions of other parametric tests. It is also robust to outliers, which makes it a useful tool in analyzing skewed or non-normal data.

Among the different research designs, experimental research design is considered the most precise because it involves controlled experiments where variables can be manipulated to observe cause-and-effect relationships.   • Involves manipulating one or more independent variables and measuring the effect on the dependent variables while controlling extraneous variables.
  • The most precise as it allows for a clear cause-and-effect relationship to be established.

Exploratory Research:

  • Used to explore a problem or issue when there are few or no previous studies to refer to.
  • It’s less precise since it’s more about gaining insights or understanding the problem broadly.

Diagnostic Research:

  • Focuses on diagnosing a problem or understanding underlying causes.
  • It’s useful for identifying problems but not as precise as experimental designs in establishing causality.

Descriptive Research:

  • Aimed at describing characteristics or functions.
  • It provides detailed information but does not manipulate variables or establish causal relationships like experimental research.

Question 36
Which of the following is an incomplete experimental research design? (UGC NET Commerce Dec 2019)
  • A.Before and after with control group design
  • B.Factorial design
  • C.One group before-after design
  • D.Latin Square design
Correct Answer: One group before-after design

Experimental Research Design: A blueprint of the procedure that enables the researcher to maintain control over all factors that may affect the result of an experiment. In doing this, the researcher attempts to determine or predict what may occur. The classic experimental design specifies an experimental group and a control group. The independent variable is administered to the experimental group and not to the control group, and both groups are measured on the same dependent variable.

 

To be classified as true experimental, there must be randomization, a control group, and manipulation of a variable when examining the direct causal or predicted relationship between variables. When any one of these requirements is not met, the design is no longer a true experiment and is classified as quasi-experimental (incomplete experimental research design). Different types of the true experimental designs are listed below:

1. Post-test Only Design: In this type of design, there are two randomly assigned groups, namely as an experimental group and a control group. None of these groups are pretested before the implementation of the treatment. The treatment is applied just to the experimental group, and the post- test is carried out on both groups to measure the effect of the treatment. When it is not possible to pretest the subjects, this type of design is commonly used.

2. Pretest, Post-Test Only Design: First, the experimental and the control group are formed, then both groups are pretested for the independent variable. After that, just the experimental group receives the treatment, and finally, both groups are post-tested to investigate the effects of the independent variable on the dependent one.

3. Solomon Four-Group Design: In this kind of design, four groups are randomly formed including two experimental groups and two control groups. Only two groups are pretested. Then, one pretested group and one un-pretested group get the treatment. All four groups receive the post-test. And, at the end, the post-test results demonstrate the effects of the dependent variable comparing to the effects of the independent variable on the dependent variable. This method is a combination of the previous two methods and potential sources of error are eliminated by using this design.

4. Factorial Design: Two or more independent variables (factors) are simultaneously manipulated to observe their effects on the dependent variable. This design allows the researcher to test two or more hypotheses in a single project.

5. Randomized Block Design: When there are intrinsic differences between subjects and possible differences in experimental conditions, this design is used. When there are a large number of experimental groups, the randomized block design makes the groups homogenous.

6. Crossover Design (also known as Repeat Measures Design): Different orders of the treatment are randomly manipulated to the subjects in this design, and they are assigned to more than one treatment. The compared groups should have an equal distribution of features and there should be a high level of similarity among the subjects. In this type of design, the subjects serve as their own control groups. Crossover designs are very good tools for doing research, but, there is something to be worried about and it is the point that the subjects’ experience with the first treatment may affect their responding to the second treatment or condition.

The term "value chain" pertains to the sequence of tasks and operations undertaken by a company to generate and deliver a product or service to its final consumers. The value chain is divided into two principal categories: primary activities and support activities. Primary activities directly relate to the creation and delivery of the product or service, while support activities are essential to facilitate the execution of primary activities.

 

Primary activities within the value chain encompass inbound logistics, operations, outbound logistics, marketing and sales, and service. Support activities within the value chain encompass procurement, technology development, human resource management, and infrastructure. Procurement entails acquiring necessary raw materials, supplies, and equipment for production. Technology development includes activities aimed at refining the production process through research and development.

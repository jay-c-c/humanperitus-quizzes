Question 2
If the spot price is higher than the strike price in a call option, it is referred to as__________.(UGC NET Commerce Jun 2013)
  • A.At-the-money
  • B.In-the-money
  • C.Out-of-the-money
  • D.Premium
Correct Answer: In-the-money

If the spot price is higher than the strike price in a call option, it is referred to as in-the-money (ITM). Understanding this concept is crucial for investors and traders in the options market, as it directly affects the potential profitability of an option.

 

A call option grants the holder the right, but not the obligation, to buy an underlying asset at a predetermined price, known as the strike price, within a specified time frame. This is a fundamental tool for investors looking to capitalize on expected increases in the price of the underlying asset.

 

A call option is considered in-the-money when the spot price (current market price) of the underlying asset is higher than the strike price. This status indicates that the option has intrinsic value. For instance, if an investor holds a call option with a strike price of Rs 50, and the current spot price of the asset is Rs 60, the option is in-the-money by Rs 10. This Rs 10 difference represents the profit per share that the option holder could realize by exercising the option and buying the asset at the lower strike price, then selling it at the higher spot price.

Question 5
Time value of an option is:(UGC NET Management Dec 2015)
  • A.Premium of the option - Intrinsic value of option
  • B.Intrinsic value of option - Price of the option
  • C.Current market price - Exercise price
  • D.Exercise price - Current market price
Correct Answer: Premium of the option - Intrinsic value of option

The time value of an option is a critical concept in options trading. It represents the portion of an option's price that exceeds its intrinsic value and reflects the probability that the option will increase in value before expiration.

 

The premium of an option is the total price that a buyer pays to acquire the option. This premium consists of two components: intrinsic value and time value.

 

Intrinsic value is the difference between the current market price of the underlying asset and the strike price of the option, but only if the option is in-the-money (for call options, it is the current market price minus the strike price; for put options, it is the strike price minus the current market price).

 

Time value is the remaining portion of the premium after subtracting the intrinsic value. It reflects the potential for the option to gain value before it expires due to changes in the underlying asset’s price, volatility, and time remaining until expiration.

Question 7
Among the following financial derivatives, which involves the least risk for the holder?(UGC NET Management Dec 2012)
  • A.Forward
  • B.Option
  • C.Futures
  • D.Swap
Correct Answer: Option

Options involve the least risk for the holder among the listed financial derivatives. Here's why:

(A) Forward: Forward contracts are private agreements between two parties to buy or sell an asset at a future date for a price agreed upon today. They carry significant risk because they are not standardized or traded on exchanges, leading to counterparty risk if one party defaults.

(B) Option: Options provide the right, but not the obligation, to buy (call option) or sell (put option) an asset at a predetermined price before a specific date. The maximum risk for the option holder is the premium paid for purchasing the option, making it a limited-risk instrument. The holder is not obliged to execute the option if it is not favorable.

(C) Futures: Futures contracts are similar to forward contracts but are standardized and traded on exchanges. They involve marking to market and margin requirements to manage risk. However, they still carry substantial risk due to daily price fluctuations and the obligation to fulfill the contract at maturity.

(D) Swap: Swaps are agreements to exchange cash flows or other financial instruments between parties. They are typically used to manage interest rate or currency risk but can involve significant risk due to the complexity and potential for large fluctuations in the value of the cash flows exchanged.

Question 8
The _____ is generally put into place when an investor believes that the price of the underlying is about to “run” but he is uncertain of the direction. It involves the purchasing of a call and a put at the same strike price and expiration date.
  • A.Strangle
  • B.Straddle
  • C.Strip
  • D.None of above
Correct Answer: Straddle

The straddle is generally put into place when an investor believes that the price of the underlying is about to “run” but he/she is uncertain of the direction. The straddle involves the purchasing of a call and a put at the same strike price and expiration date. This strategy allows the investor to make a profit regardless of whether the price of the security goes up or down, assuming the change in the underlying stock price is significant enough to move past either of the strike prices and offset the cost of the premiums.

 

A strangle is the same as the straddle except that the exercise prices differ. Thus, a strangle is an option strategy in which a call and put with the same expiration date but different strikes is bought.

 

Straddle provides equal profit potential on either side of underlying price movement (making it a “perfect” market neutral strategy), while the Strip is a “bearish” market neutral strategy providing double the profit potential on downward price move compared to equivalent upward price move. Strip Options offer unlimited profit potential on the upward price movement of the underlying, and limited profit potential on downward price movement. The risk/loss is limited to the total option premium paid (plus brokerage & commission).

This strategy is employed to mitigate potential losses resulting from adverse and unexpected market movements. Correct Answer: Delta

Delta is the degree to which an option price will move given a small change in the underlying stock price. It is also known as hedge ratio. For example, an option with a delta of 0.5 will move half a rupee for every full rupee movement in the underlying stock.

 

A deeply out-of-the-money call will have a delta very close to zero; a deeply in-the-money call will have a delta very close to 1. Call deltas are positive and put deltas are negative.

 

Gamma measures how fast the delta changes for small changes in the underlying stock price i.e. delta of the delta.

Theta is the change in option price given a one day decrease in time to expiration. It is a measure of time decay.

In Securitization when no assets are acquired and the collateral is fixed for the life of the asset, the type of a structure is called as (UGC NET Commerce Jun 2021)
  • A.Revolving structure

  • D.Self-liquidating structure
Correct Answer: Self-liquidating structure

Securitization involves pooling together various types of debt instruments, such as loans or receivables, and converting them into tradable securities. These securities are then sold to investors, allowing the originator of the debt to raise funds. In securitization, the collateral backing these securities can be either amortizing or non-amortizing assets.

 

Amortizing Assets

Amortizing assets are loans for which borrowers make scheduled payments consisting of both principal and interest over the life of the loan.

A typical example of an amortizing asset is a residential mortgage loan. Each payment made by the borrower includes both interest on the outstanding balance and a portion of the principal.

The repayment schedule for the principal, known as the amortization schedule, outlines how the loan balance decreases over time with each payment.

 

Non-Amortizing Assets

Non-amortizing assets do not have a set schedule for principal repayments like amortizing assets. Instead, borrowers make minimum periodic payments, and any shortfall is added to the outstanding loan balance.

An example of a non-amortizing asset is credit card receivables. Cardholders make minimum payments, but if these payments do not cover the full interest, the remaining amount is added to the outstanding balance.

 

Self-Liquidating Structure

When securitizing amortizing assets, the collateral remains fixed over the life of the structure. This means that no new assets are acquired except in cases of prepayments or defaults.

The term "self-liquidating" refers to the fact that the structure gradually pays down its debt over time as borrowers make scheduled payments. The structure is self-sustaining and does not require additional assets to maintain collateral.

Investors receive payments from the underlying loans as they are repaid, and these payments are used to service the securities issued against the collateral.

Question 21
Which of the following events contributed most to the disruption in the Bretton Woods system? (UGC NET Management Dec 2022)
  • A.Introduction of Special Drawing Rights
  • B.Western dominance over the system

  • D.Oil price shock of 1973
Correct Answer: Suspension of convertibility of dollar into gold in 1971

The event that contributed most to the disruption in the Bretton Woods system was the (C) Suspension of convertibility of the dollar into gold in 1971. This event is commonly referred to as the "Nixon Shock."

 

Before this significant event, the Bretton Woods system operated with the US dollar as the world's primary reserve currency, and its value was tied to a fixed amount of gold. Other countries held large quantities of US dollars as part of their foreign exchange reserves, with the confidence that they could exchange their dollars for gold at the established fixed rate.

 

However, in 1971, President Richard Nixon announced the suspension of the dollar's convertibility into gold. This decision had profound implications for the international monetary system. It eroded confidence in the stability of the US dollar and, by extension, the entire Bretton Woods system. With the convertibility of the dollar into gold no longer assured, the system's credibility was undermined.

 

The suspension of convertibility triggered a series of currency crises, exchange rate fluctuations, and increased uncertainty in international financial markets. As a result, countries began to abandon the fixed exchange rate regime, and the Bretton Woods system eventually collapsed in 1973 when currencies were allowed to float freely against each other.

  • C.Interest rate parity
  • D.International Fisher effect
Correct Answer: Interest rate parity

The concept described is known as Interest Rate Parity (IRP).

 

Interest rate parity (IRP) is a theory in finance that suggests that the difference between the forward exchange rate and the spot exchange rate is equal to the interest rate differential between two currencies. In other words, it states that the forward premium or discount on a currency should reflect the difference in interest rates between the two countries.

This concept plays a crucial role in the foreign exchange market and helps determine forward exchange rates. It also has implications for international investment decisions and can influence capital flows between countries.

The three common forms of arbitrage in the context of foreign exchange and international money markets are:

Fiat Currency:

A fiat currency is a type of currency that a government declares to be legal tender, meaning it must be accepted for the payment of debts within the country. Unlike commodity money, which is backed by a physical commodity such as gold or silver, fiat money has no intrinsic value. Its value is not based on the material from which it is made (such as paper or metal). Instead, the value of fiat money is derived from the trust and confidence that people have in the issuing government and its ability to maintain the currency's value. The primary factors influencing the value of fiat money are supply and demand, as well as the stability and economic health of the issuing country.

 

Clean Float:

A clean float, also known as an independent floating exchange rate regime, refers to a situation where a country's currency value is determined solely by market forces without direct government or central bank intervention. In a clean float system, the exchange rate fluctuates freely based on supply and demand in the foreign exchange market. This means that factors such as trade balances, interest rates, and economic indicators influence the currency's value. A clean float allows for natural adjustments in the exchange rate in response to changes in the global economy.

 

Managed Float:

A managed float, or dirty float, is an exchange rate regime where the currency value is allowed to fluctuate in response to market forces, but the central bank or government occasionally intervenes to stabilize or influence the exchange rate. This intervention typically involves buying or selling the domestic currency in the foreign exchange market to maintain the currency within a desired range or to achieve specific economic objectives. The degree of intervention can vary, and the primary goal is often to reduce excessive volatility or to address imbalances in the currency's value that could negatively impact the economy.

 

Free-Floating Currency:

A free-floating currency is one whose value is entirely determined by market forces of supply and demand without any direct intervention by the country's central bank or government. In this regime, the exchange rate can fluctuate widely based on changes in economic conditions, investor sentiment, and other factors influencing the foreign exchange market. A free-floating currency system allows for automatic adjustments in the exchange rate in response to shifts in trade balances, capital flows, and other economic variables. While this system can lead to significant volatility, it also reflects the true market value of the currency at any given time.

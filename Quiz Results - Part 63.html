Spurious relationships can be avoided by carefully considering potential third variables that may be related to both of the variables being analyzed. A spurious relationship is a statistical relationship between two variables that appears to be true but is actually the result of a third variable that is related to both. In other words, a spurious relationship is a coincidence rather than a true causal relationship between the two variables.

For example, suppose there is a positive correlation between ice cream sales and the number of drownings in a city. One might be tempted to conclude that eating ice cream causes people to drown, but this is not the case. The spurious relationship is actually due to a third variable, temperature. Both ice cream sales and the number of drownings are related to temperature, with warmer temperatures leading to more ice cream sales and more people going swimming, resulting in more drownings.

In this case, the spurious relationship between ice cream sales and drowning disappears when temperature is taken into account. Temperature is the true causal variable that affects both ice cream sales and the number of drownings, and any relationship between ice cream sales and drowning is coincidental.

Multicollinearityin the regression analysis is measured by: (UGC NET Commerce Jun 2022)
  • A.Tolerance (1 -R2)
  • B.Homoscedasticity
  • C.Partial F-test
  • D.Durbin-whatsonstatistic
Correct Answer: Tolerance (1 -R2)

In regression analysis, multicollinearity refers to the presence of high correlation or linear dependence among the independent variables. It can cause issues in the interpretation of the regression model and lead to unreliable coefficient estimates.

 

One way to measure multicollinearity is by using the tolerance statistic, which is calculated as (1 - R2), where R² is the coefficient of determination of a regression model with the independent variables as predictors.

 

The tolerance statistic measures the proportion of the variance of an independent variable that is not explained by the other independent variables in the model. It quantifies the extent to which an independent variable is linearly related to the other independent variables in the model.

 

A tolerance value close to 1 indicates low multicollinearity, meaning that the independent variable is not highly correlated with the other variables and contributes unique information to the model. On the other hand, a tolerance value close to 0 indicates high multicollinearity, suggesting that the independent variable is highly correlated with the other variables and does not contribute much unique information to the model.

 

By calculating (1 - R2) for each independent variable, you can assess the extent of multicollinearity in the regression model. A low tolerance value indicates high multicollinearity, suggesting that the variable is redundant or highly correlated with other variables in the model. In such cases, it may be necessary to address multicollinearity, either by removing redundant variables, transforming variables, or using other techniques such as ridge regression or principal component analysis.

Question 10
‘Average clause’ in insurance is applicable for which one of the following cases? (UGC NET Commerce Dec 2022)
  • A.Double Insurance
  • B.Re-insurance
  • C.Over insurance
  • D.Under insurance
Correct Answer: Under insurance

The correct answer is option D i.e., Under Insurance.

 

The “average clause” in insurance is a provision that applies when the insured has not insured the full value of the property. It allows the insurance company to reduce the amount of the claim in proportion to the under-insurance.

 

For example, let’s say that a person insures their house for Rs. 50 lakhs, but the actual value of the house is Rs. 75 lakhs. If the house is damaged in a fire and the cost to repair the damage is Rs. 10 lakhs, the insurance company would only pay out 2/3 of the claim (i.e., Rs. 6.67 lakh) because the insured was under-insured by 1/3.

 

The purpose of the average clause is to encourage the insured to insure their property for its full value, and to prevent them from taking out insurance for a lower amount and paying lower premiums. This ensures that the insurance company receives adequate compensation for the risks it is undertaking, and that the insured is adequately protected in the event of a loss.

General Insurance Corporation: A Brief History

The entire general insurance business in India was nationalized by General Insurance Business (Nationalization) Act, 1972 (GIBNA).

 

The Government of India (GOI), through Nationalization took over the shares of Indian insurance companies and the undertakings of insurers carrying on general insurance business and thus, General Insurance Corporation of India (GIC) was formed in pursuance of Section 9(1) of GIBNA. It was incorporated on 22 November 1972 under the Companies Act, 1956 as a private company limited by shares.

 

GIC was formed for the purpose of superintending, controlling and carrying on the business of general insurance. As soon as GIC was formed, GoI transferred all the shares it held of the general insurance companies to GIC. Simultaneously, the nationalized undertakings were transferred to Indian insurance companies. After a process of mergers among Indian insurance companies, four companies were left as fully owned subsidiary companies of GIC. These were:

 

1. National Insurance Company Limited.

2. The New India Assurance Company Limited

3.The Oriental Insurance Company Limited.

4. United India Insurance Company Limited.

 

The next landmark happened on 19th April 2000, when the Insurance Regulatory and Development Authority Act, 1999 (IRDAA) came into force. This Act also introduced amendment to GIBNA and the Insurance Act, 1938. An amendment to GIBNA removed the exclusive privilege of GIC and its subsidiaries carrying on general insurance in India. With the General Insurance Business (Nationalization) Amendment Act 2002 (40 of 2002) coming into force from March 21, 2003; GIC ceased to be a holding company of its subsidiaries. Premium loading reflects insurer administrative and capital costs. If an insurance contract’s premium equals the present value of expected claim costs, a risk-averse person will likely demand full insurance coverage for monetary losses that otherwise would be paid by the person. Because premiums almost always have a positive loading, however, risk-averse people will demand less than full coverage. Thus, any factor that increases administrative or capital cost (and thus the loading on a policy) will limit the amount of private market insurance coverage. Simply stated, higher premium loading generally imply less coverage. In addition, insurance coverage for some risk exposures is likely to be extremely limited or even nonexistent because of administrative and capital costs.

Question 25
An insurer charges different premiums from two types of consumers. Moreover, the consumers have different expected losses. This situation is called
  • A.Adverse Selection
  • B.Moral Hazard
  • C.Risk avoidance
  • D.None of the above
Correct Answer: Adverse Selection

Adverse Selection - Another factor that limits the insurability of risk is adverse selection. It refers to situations in which consumers have different expected losses (for example, book worms versus skateboarders), but the insurer is unable to distinguish between the two types of consumers and charge them different premiums. If an insurer offers insurance at the same price to heterogeneous consumers and consumers know their expected losses, then the higher expected loss consumers (skateboarders) will tend to purchase more insurance coverage relative to the case where insureds are charged premium based on their expected losses. Conversely, the lower expected loss consumers (bookworms) will tend to purchase less insurance coverage. When this scenario occurs, adverse selection takes place.

 

Thus, adverse selection arises because it is too costly to classify insureds perfectly. Indeed, if each consumer’s expected claim costs could be observed costless, then insurance premiums (barring government restrictions) would vary exactly with expected claim costs and there would be no adverse selection.

 

A good example of adverse selection is in the health insurance market. People most likely to purchase health insurance are those who are most likely to use it, i.e. smokers/drinkers/those with underlying health issues. The insurance company knows this and so raises the average price of insurance cover. This risks pricing healthy consumers out of the market, meaning that only high risk individuals gain insurance – this is clearly a market failure. One interpretation of adverse selection is that "we tend to trust the people we shouldn't!"

 

Moral hazard refers to the effect of insurance on the insured’s incentives to reduce expected losses. Since losses are incurred, a portion of the benefits accrues to the insurer in the form of lower expected claim costs. The two conditions are required for moral hazard to arise. First, expected losses must depend on the insured’s behavior after having obtained insurance. Second, it must be costly for the insurer to observe precautions by policy holders and measure their impact on expected claim costs.

 

For example: You have not insured your house from any future damages. It implies that a loss will be completely borne by you at the time of a mishappening like fire or burglary. Hence you will show extra care and attentiveness. You will install high tech burglar alarms and hire watchmen to avoid any unforeseen event. But if your house is insured for its full value, then if anything happens you do not really lose anything. Therefore, you have less incentive to protect against any mishappening. In this case, the insurance firm bears the losses and the problem of moral hazard arises.

 

Risk avoidance is one method of handling risk. For example, you can avoid the risk of being pick pocketed in Delhi Metro by not travelling in Metro. Similarly, you can avoid the risk of divorce by not marrying.

Randomness: Insurance is based on the principle of randomness, which means that losses can happen at any time and to any individual. This is why insurance is so important, as it helps to manage the financial risk of these unpredictable events.

Question 56

  • B.Constructive Total Loss
  • C.Construction Totally Lost
  • D.Contractors’ Total Loss
Correct Answer: Constructive Total Loss

CTL as used in insurance is Constructive Total Loss.

 

In insurance, "Constructive Total Loss" (CTL) refers to a situation where the cost of repairing damaged property is greater than the value of the property itself. In other words, if the repair costs are so high that it would not make economic sense to repair the property, it is considered a Constructive Total Loss.

 

A CTL is important in insurance because it triggers the payment of a claim by the insurance company. If a policyholder has insurance coverage for the damaged property and it is determined to be a CTL, the insurance company will pay out the full value of the policy to the policyholder, less any applicable deductibles. This payment can be used to purchase a replacement for the damaged property or for other expenses as deemed necessary by the policyholder.

 

It's important to note that a CTL determination is made by the insurance company based on an evaluation of the repair costs versus the value of the property, and the decision can be influenced by factors such as the value of the property, the extent of the damage, and the cost of repairs. In some cases, the policyholder may dispute the insurance company's determination of a CTL and seek further compensation.

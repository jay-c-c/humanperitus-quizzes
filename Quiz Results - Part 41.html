The concepts of 'Hard HRM' and 'Soft HRM' were developed by John Storey. These concepts represent two different approaches to human resource management:

Question 5
Which model of H.R.M. propagates that, “if integrated set of HRM practices is applied in a coherent fashion, with a view to achieving the normative goals of high commitment, high quality and task flexibility; then superior individual performance will result; which would lead to superior organizational performance”? (UGC NET HRM Jul 2016)
  • A.Harvard model
  • B.Warwick model
  • C.David Guest model
  • D.John M. Storey model
Correct Answer: David Guest model

The model of Human Resource Management (HRM) that propagates the idea that if an integrated set of HRM practices is applied in a coherent manner to achieve the normative goals of high commitment, high quality, and task flexibility, superior individual performance will result, leading to superior organizational performance, is known as the David Guest model.

 

Developed by David Guest in 1997, the Guest model is considered a 'Soft' HRM model. It emphasizes the importance of designing HR policies and practices to achieve four primary outcomes: strategic integration, high employee commitment to the organization, high workforce flexibility and adaptability, and a high-quality workforce. Guest argues that when these HRM outcomes are achieved, it results in high job performance, effective problem-solving, change consistent with strategic goals, and improved cost-effectiveness. This model suggests that a coherent strategy of HRM policies fully integrated into the business strategy, supported by all levels of line management, is necessary for these outcomes to be realized.

 

In contrast, the other models—such as the Harvard model, Michigan model, Warwick model, and John M. Storey's hard and soft HRM distinctions—propose different perspectives on HRM focusing on various aspects, including human aspects, resource aspects, and their integration with organizational strategy and policies. Each model brings unique approaches to managing the workforce, differing in their emphasis on factors such as control, human elements, and their role in achieving organizational success.

  • A.Harvard model
  • B.Warwick model
  • C.David Guest model
  • D.John M. Storey model
Correct Answer: Warwick model

Warwick Model was developed by two researchers, Hendry and Pettigrew of university of Warwick (hence the name Warwick model).

The Warwick proposition centres around five elements;

1) Outer Context (macro environmental forces),

2) Inner Context (firm specific or micro environmental forces),

3) Business Strategy Content,

4) HRM Context and

5) HRM Content.

This model takes cognizance of business strategy and HR practices (as the Guest Model), the external and internal context (unlike the Guest Model), in which these activities take place, and the process by which such changes take place, including interactions between changes in both context and content.

Question 10
Who had advanced the ‘soft’ and ‘hard’ approach of HRM? (UGC NET HRM Sep 2013)
  • A.Benjamin Schneider
  • B.James Niniger
  • C.John Storey
  • D.Lloyd Baind
Correct Answer: John Storey

John Storey (1989) distinguished between the 'hard' and 'soft' versions of Human Resource Management (HRM), each emphasizing different aspects of managing people within organizations.

 

Hard HRM is characterized by a more traditional and transactional perspective. It views employees as resources similar to any other input in the production process. In this approach, the primary focus is on efficiency, cost control, and achieving organizational goals. Human resources are managed in a manner that aligns closely with business objectives, often emphasizing measurable outcomes and performance metrics. The Position Analysis Questionnaire (PAQ) was developed by McCormick and associates (1972) on the assumption that there is an underlying taxonomy to all jobs. That is, in contract to the other methods, the PAQ approach focuses on broad categories common to all jobs rather than on individual elements of specific jobs. Given the thousands of tasks for one job that the other methods may identify, PAQ attempts to put this data into a more manageable form. PAQ reduces all jobs to 194 elements, which are classified in terms of six broader dimensions.

These jobs are well-established positions within the organization that are clearly understood and can serve as reference points for comparing other jobs. Benchmark jobs are usually stable and commonly found across different departments.

 

The next step is to select compensable factors. These factors represent the key elements of a job for which the organization is willing to pay. Common compensable factors include skills, responsibilities, effort, and working conditions. The selection of these factors often depends on industry norms and organizational traditions.

 

Once compensable factors are identified, the third step is to assign weights to these factors. Weighting indicates the importance of each factor in relation to the others. Higher weights are given to factors considered more significant for achieving organizational goals.

Question 26
The process of reorganizing a company’s employees to improve their efficiency is termed as: (UGC NET Management Jun 2014)
  • A.Right sizing
  • B.Down sizing
  • C.Retrenchment
  • D.Lay-offs
Correct Answer: Right sizing

The process of reorganizing a company’s employees to improve their efficiency is termed as Right sizing.

 

These terms are explained below:

 

1. Right sizing: This process involves reorganizing a company's workforce or structure to enhance efficiency and effectiveness. It's about aligning the number of employees, their skills, and departments to better meet the organization's needs. Unlike downsizing, which focuses on reducing workforce irrespective of efficiency, right sizing aims to create an optimal and efficient structure within the organization.

2. Downsizing: This refers to the permanent reduction of a company's workforce, often involving the elimination of unproductive or surplus workers or departments. It's usually associated with economic challenges or strategic changes within the company. Downsizing aims to reduce costs but might adversely affect morale and productivity if not managed properly.

3. Retrenchment: This term usually signifies the involuntary separation of employees due to various reasons such as technological advancements leading to job redundancies or the closure of specific departments or divisions. It generally involves the replacement of human labor with machinery or automation.

4. Lay-offs: A lay-off is the temporary separation of employees from their jobs due to a lack of work. It implies that the termination is not necessarily permanent and might be recalled back to work if the situation improves. However, it might eventually become a permanent termination depending on the organization's circumstances.

 

Each of these terms relates to workforce restructuring, but they differ in their objectives, permanency, and the circumstances under which they occur

Question 33
Arrange the following steps of developing Behaviorally Anchored Rating Scales (BARS) in the right sequence: (UGC NET Commerce Jul 2016)(a) Developing performance dimensions(b) Generate critical incidents(c) Scale the incidents(d) Reallocate the incidents(e) Develop the final instrument

  • D.(a), (c), (b), (d), (e)
Correct Answer: (b), (a), (d), (c), (e)

Behaviorally Anchored Rating Scales (BARS) is a technique used for Performance Appraisal.

A BARS combines the benefits of narratives critical incidents and quantified ratings by anchoring a quantified scale with specific behavioral examples of a good or poor performance. BARS was proposed by Charles L Hulin and Milton R Blood. Developing BARS typically requires the following five steps:

 

1. Generate critical incidents: Person who knows the job being appraised is asked to describe specific illustrations of effective and ineffective performance.

2. Develop performance dimensions: These people then cluster the incidents into a smaller set of performance dimensions. Each cluster is then defined.

3. Reallocate incidents: Another group of people who also know the job then reallocate the original critical incidents. They are given the clusters’ definitions and the critical incidents and are asked to reassign each incident to they think it fits best.

4. Scale the incidents: This second group is generally asked to rate the behavior described in the incident as to how effectively or ineffectively it represents performance on the appropriate dimension.

5. Develop final instrument: A subset of the incidents is used as behavioral anchors for each dimension.

Under the Global Approach, multinational companies (MNCs) determine uniform pay scales for specific jobs across all countries where they operate. This approach is based on the belief that certain jobs require the same level of skills, knowledge, and competencies worldwide. As a result, the compensation structure for these positions remains consistent, irrespective of the employee’s nationality or location.

 

This approach applies uniformly to all categories of employees, including host country nationals, parent country nationals, and third-country nationals. The idea is to promote fairness and equity among employees of different nationalities working in different geographical locations.

 

The global approach also considers additional allowances similar to those given within national contexts, such as climate allowances in difficult regions or danger allowances in conflict zones. Different Approaches to International Compensation Management

 

1. Salary Level in Host Country (Localization Approach)

Under this approach, MNCs determine compensation based on the salary levels of similar jobs in comparable organizations in the host country. For instance, HSBC in India decides pay scales for Indians, Chinese, and Americans working in India based on salary levels of Indian banks like ICICI and HDFC, or foreign banks like Citibank operating in India. This ensures alignment with the prevailing salary standards of the host country.

 

2. Negotiation/Bargaining Approach

This approach involves mutual negotiation between the employee and the employer to finalize compensation. Once agreed, neither party generally has grievances until the contract period ends. However, both may face disappointment if actual conditions in the host country differ from expectations. Host country nationals may also perceive inequality under this method.

 

3. Lump Sum Approach

Here, the MNC decides the total compensation package as a single amount, covering salary, allowances, and benefits. The employee has the freedom to allocate this money towards housing, medical needs, transportation, air travel, recreation, taxation, savings repatriation, and other requirements.

 

4. Buffet Approach

This approach provides employees with flexibility to choose between cash and various benefit components within a pre-decided salary package. Expatriates often select more benefits to reduce tax burdens and maximize savings. Although it involves higher administrative costs, it caters well to individual employee needs.

 

5. Cluster Systems Approach

Under this system, MNCs group countries or cities into clusters based on cost of living, hardships, and safety conditions. A uniform compensation package is given within each cluster, with additional allowances for unique personal circumstances. If employees are transferred to different clusters, compensation adjustments are made accordingly.

 

6. Global Approach

In the global approach, MNCs maintain uniform pay scales for certain jobs worldwide where skill and competency requirements are similar. It applies equally to all categories of employees. Additional allowances, like climate or danger allowances, may be included based on location-specific challenges, ensuring fairness and equality across regions.

 

7. Performance-Based Compensation Approach

This approach links a portion of the compensation package to employee performance. It motivates employees to perform better and allows top performers to earn higher salaries, regardless of their nationality. A minimum base salary is guaranteed for all to meet essential needs, making this approach both employee- and organization-friendly.

 

8. Double Home Country Salary and Living Compensation at Host Country

MNCs using this approach provide expatriates with double the best domestic salary they could earn in their home country, plus living compensation in the host country. The double salary portion is typically paid in home country currency, reducing tax burden and safeguarding against currency fluctuations.

 

9. Balance Sheet Approach

This approach aims to balance various elements of compensation, including home country salary, sacrifices, inconveniences, hardships, and host country challenges. It is designed to offer an attractive and comprehensive package, especially for mid- and senior-level expatriates, ensuring their comfort and satisfaction in international assignments.

Question 22
Taylor differential piece rate system is related to: (UGC NET Management Jun 2012)
  • A.Time wage system
  • B.Piece wage system
  • C.Incentive wage system
  • D.None of the above
Correct Answer: Incentive wage system

The Taylor differential piece rate system is a part of the incentive wage system. It's a method devised by F.W. Taylor, a pioneer in scientific management, aiming to rationalize incentives for workers based on their efficiency. The core aspects of this system involve the use of piece rates, predetermined standard outputs, and two different piece rates—one higher and one lower.

 

The system operates with the assumption that worker efficiency varies, and thus, employees should be compensated based on their respective levels of efficiency. Workers who meet or surpass the predetermined standard output receive wages at a higher piece rate (e.g., 120% of the standard rate), while those who fall below the standard receive wages at a lower piece rate (e.g., 80% of the standard rate). Unlike some systems, minimum wages are not guaranteed under this model.

Gagne-Briggs Theory of Training is otherwise called as Instructional Design Theory.

 

Gagné suggested (Instructional Design Theory) 9 universal steps of instruction which should be found in any instructional context: 

1) Gain attention: e.g. present a good problem, a new situation, use a multimedia advertisement. 

2)  Describe the goal: e.g. describe the goal of a lesson (task,...), state what students will be able to accomplish and how they will be able to use the knowledge, give a demonstration if appropriate. 

3) Stimulate recall of prior knowledge: e.g. remind the student of prior knowledge relevant to the current lesson (facts, rules, procedures or skills). Show how knowledge is connected, provide the student with a framework that helps learning and remembering. Tests can be included. 

4) Present the material to be learned: e.g. text, graphics, simulations, figures, pictures, sound, etc. e.g. follow a consistent presentation style, chunking of information (avoid memory overload, recall information). 

5) Provide guidance for learning: e.g. presentation of content is different from instructions on how to learn. Should be simpler and easier that content. Use of different channel.

6) Elicit performance "practice", let the learner do something with the newly acquired behavior, practice skills or apply knowledge. 

7) Provide informative feedback show correctness of the trainee's response, analyze learner's behavior (or let him do it), maybe present a good (step-by-step) solution of the problem. 

8)   Assess performance test, if the lesson has been learned. also give sometimes general progress information. 

9) Enhance retention and transfer inform the learner about similar problem situations, provide additional practice. Put the learner in a transfer situation. Maybe let the learner review the lesson.

The theory specifies four primary presentation forms: rules (expository presentation of a generality), examples (expository presentation of instances), recall (inquisitory generality) and practice (inquisitory instance). Correct Answer: Reaction, learning, behaviour and results

Donald Kirkpatrick's model of training evaluation is structured around four levels, each addressing different aspects of the training process. Here's a breakdown of these levels:

 

1. Reaction: This level measures how participants react to the training program. It involves gathering feedback on their perceptions, satisfaction, and attitudes towards the training. Essentially, it evaluates the trainees' immediate reactions and responses to the training experience.

 

2. Learning: This level assesses the increase in knowledge, skills, or capabilities that participants have acquired as a result of the training. It looks at whether the intended learning outcomes have been achieved and measures the change in understanding or competence.

 

3. Behaviour: This level focuses on whether the trainees have implemented what they've learned into their actual job roles or applied the acquired knowledge and skills. It evaluates the transfer of learning from the training environment to the workplace.

 

4. Results: The final level of evaluation considers the broader impact of the training on the organization. It measures the effectiveness of the training in terms of its impact on business outcomes, such as increased productivity, reduced costs, improved quality, or enhanced performance.

Donald Kirkpatrick's model of training evaluation comprises four levels:

 

1. Reaction: This level pertains to the initial reaction and response of the trainees to the training program. It measures their thoughts, feelings, and overall satisfaction with the training.

The Phillips ROI Model is a methodology and process to tie the costs of training programs with their actual results.

4. Business Results: Measures business impact of the program.

 

5. Return on Investment: Measures the monetary value of the results and costs for the program, usually expressed as a percentage.

Question 1
Which of the following curves are envelope curves?1. Long run average cost curve2. Long run marginal cost curve3. Long run total cost curveCodes:
  • A.1 and 2
  • B.2 and 3
  • C.1 and 3
  • D.1, 2 and 3
Correct Answer: 1 and 3

Envelope curves, also known as "enveloping curves," are curves that touch or envelope a family of other curves, such that at every point on the envelope curve, it touches one of the curves in the family. In other words, the envelope curve represents the boundary or outer limit of the family of curves it envelops.

 

Long run average cost curve: This curve represents the average cost per unit of output in the long run for various levels of production. It is often shaped like a U-curve, showing economies of scale at lower levels of production and diseconomies of scale at higher levels. The Long Run Average Cost (LRAC) curve is considered an envelope curve because it encompasses or envelopes a range of short-run average cost curves (SRAC) for different plant sizes.

 

Long run total cost curve: This curve shows the total cost incurred by a firm in the long run for various levels of production. It also envelops a range of short-run total cost curves (SRTC) for different plant sizes. The Long Run Total Cost (LRTC) curve represents the boundary for the total cost incurred at different levels of production when the firm can vary its plant size.

 

The Long Run Marginal Cost (LRMC) curve, on the other hand, is not typically considered an envelope curve because it represents the additional cost incurred by producing one more unit in the long run and does not envelop a family of other curves like the LRAC and LRTC curves do.

 

So, in the given options, both the Long Run Average Cost curve and the Long Run Total Cost curve are considered envelope curves, and the correct answer is (C) 1 and 3.

Question 2
The shape of Average Fixed Cost (AFC) curve in the short run is
  • A.Rectangular hyperbola
  • B.U shaped
  • C.S shaped
  • D.None of the above
Correct Answer: Rectangular hyperbola

The shape of the Average Fixed Cost (AFC) curve in the short run is a continuously declining curve that approaches zero as the level of output increases. It is often described as a "rectangular hyperbola" because it has a rectangular shape when plotted on a graph, and it approaches but never reaches the x-axis (zero AFC) as output increases.

 

So, the correct answer is (A) Rectangular hyperbola.

Question 3
The reason behind inverse S shape of Total Variable Cost (TVC) curve is
  • A.Law of variable proportion
  • B.Law of returns to scale
  • C.Law of diminishing marginal utility
  • D.None of the above
Correct Answer: Law of variable proportion

The inverse S shape of the Total Variable Cost (TVC) curve is primarily driven by the Law of Variable Proportions. This law states that as a firm increases the variable input (such as labor or raw materials) while keeping at least one input fixed, there is an initial stage of increasing marginal returns, where each additional unit of the variable input contributes more to output. This leads to a decline in TVC per unit of output, causing the TVC curve to slope downwards. However, beyond a certain point, diminishing marginal returns set in, where each additional unit of the variable input contributes less to output, causing TVC per unit of output to rise. This results in the upward-sloping portion of the TVC curve, creating the characteristic inverse S shape.

Question 4
When average cost falls as a result of an increase in output, marginal cost is _______ than average cost.
  • A.Less
  • B.More
  • C.Equal
  • D.None of the above
Correct Answer: Less

The relationship between marginal cost and average cost is the same as that between any other marginal-average quantities. The following are the points of relationship between the two.

(1) When average cost falls as a result of an increase in output, marginal cost is less than average cost.

(2) When average cost rises as a result of an increase in output, marginal cost is more than average cost.

(3) When average cost is minimum, marginal cost is equal to the average cost. In other words, marginal cost curve cuts average cost curve at its minimum point (i.e. optimum point).

Question 6
The minimum point of ATC curve will always occur to the right of the minimum point of the _______ curve.
  • A.TVC
  • B.MC
  • C.AFC
  • D.AVC
Correct Answer: AVC

Average total cost (ATC), is the sum of average variable cost and average fixed cost. i.e., ATC = AFC + AVC. It is the total cost divided by the number of units produced, i.e. ATC = TC/Q. The behaviour of average total cost curve depends upon the behaviour of the average variable cost curve and the average fixed cost curve. In the beginning, both AVC and AFC curves fall, therefore, the ATC curve will also fall sharply. When AVC curve begins to rise, but AFC curve still falls steeply, ATC curve continues to fall. This is because, during this stage, the fall in AFC curve is greater than the rise in the AVC curve, but as output increases further, there is a sharp rise in AVC which more than offsets the fall in AFC. Therefore, ATC curve first falls, reaches its minimum and then rises. Thus, the average total cost curve is a “U” shaped curve. The minimum point of ATC curve will always occur to the right of the minimum point of the AVC curve.

Question 7
Which of the following are the causes of shift in cost curves?1. Change in input supply2. Change in technology3. Change in preferences of consumersCodes:
  • A.1 and 2
  • B.2 and 3
  • C.1 and 3
  • D.1, 2 and 3
Correct Answer: 1 and 2

Shifts in cost curves can be caused by various factors, and here are some of the key causes:

 

Change in input supply: When the availability or cost of inputs, such as labor or raw materials, changes, it can lead to shifts in cost curves. For example, if the price of a variable input like wages increases, all cost curves (Total Cost, Average Total Cost, and Average Variable Cost) tend to shift upward. These shifts maintain their original shape and characteristics, with the Minimum Average Total Cost (ATC) and Minimum Average Variable Cost (AVC) still intersected by the Marginal Cost (MC) curve at their respective minimum points, and the difference between ATC and AVC remaining as Average Fixed Cost (AFC).

 

Change in technology: Technological advancements can also cause shifts in cost curves. Improved technology can lead to increased productivity, reducing the cost of production. As a result, one or more of the cost curves (Total Cost, Average Total Cost, Average Variable Cost) shift downward. The specific nature of the shift depends on whether technology primarily enhances labor productivity, capital productivity, or a combination of both.

 

Changes in consumer preferences typically do not directly cause shifts in cost curves. Cost curves are more influenced by production factors and technology. Consumer preferences may impact the demand curve, affecting the price and quantity produced, but they are not a direct cause of shifts in cost curves.

 

So, in the context of the question, the causes of shifts in cost curves are primarily related to changes in input supply and technology, which are represented by options (A) 1 and 2.

Question 10
What is the reason behind the U shape of MC curve?
  • A.Law of variable proportions
  • B.Law of returns to scale
  • C.Law of diminishing marginal utility
  • D.None of the above
Correct Answer: Law of variable proportions

The U shape of the MC curve reflects the effects of the Law of Variable Proportions. Initially, as more units of the variable input are added, MC declines. This corresponds to the downward-sloping part of the U-shaped curve. In this phase, producing additional units becomes cheaper on average.

 

However, beyond a certain point, the Law of Diminishing Marginal Returns sets in. This means that the additional units of the variable input start to contribute less and less to total output, resulting in an increase in MC. This corresponds to the upward-sloping part of the U-shaped curve. During this phase, producing extra units becomes more costly per unit, as resources become less efficiently utilized

Concentration ratios are used as measures of monopoly power in industries. Lerner index, in economics, is a measure of the market power of a firm. Formalized by the Russian-British economist Abba P. Lerner in 1934, the Lerner index is expressed in the following formula:

Lerner index = (P – MC)/P

 

Where, P represents the price of the good set by the firm and MC represents the firm’s marginal cost.

The Stackelberg model is a framework used to analyze oligopolistic markets, and it is often described as the "first mover advantage model." This model is a variation of the Cournot model, which deals with how firms in an oligopoly decide their output levels. The key distinction in the Stackelberg model is that it introduces a sequential decision-making process among the firms, as opposed to the simultaneous decision-making in the Cournot model.

 

Developed by German economist Heinrich von Stackelberg in 1934, the Stackelberg model assumes that one firm, known as the Stackelberg leader, takes the first move by deciding its output level. This leader then anticipates the response of the other firm, referred to as the Stackelberg follower. The critical concept here is that the leader firm gains an advantage because it can factor in the expected reaction of its competitor when making its own production and pricing decisions.

 

In this model, the leader's output decision is based on its knowledge of the follower's reaction function, which shows how the follower will adjust its output in response to the leader's decision. Conversely, the follower's output decision is based on its understanding of the leader's output decision, considering the leader's assumption about the follower's reaction function. This mutual interdependence between firms makes the Stackelberg model distinct from the Cournot model, where firms independently determine their output levels.

  • B.B, C and D only
  • C.C, D and E only
  • D.A, B and D only
Correct Answer: A, B and D only

Cost-Volume-Profit (CVP) analysis is a valuable tool for profit planning and decision-making, yet it comes with several limitations that need to be acknowledged for its effective application.

 

Here are some key limitations:

  • Linear Relationship Assumption: CVP analysis assumes a linear relationship between costs and volume, overlooking factors like economies of scale, learning curves, and discounts that can alter unit costs with varying volumes. This oversight may lead to inaccurate cost and profit estimations.
  • Constant Sales Mix Assumption: The analysis assumes a constant sales mix, disregarding potential changes in product proportions due to market demand, competition, or pricing strategies. This can result in misleading overall profit and break-even point calculations.
  • Constant Selling Price Assumption: CVP analysis assumes a constant selling price, neglecting the impact of price fluctuations, discounts, and promotions on product revenues. This may result in overstated or understated profit estimations.
  • Constant Inventory Levels Assumption: It assumes constant inventory levels, overlooking fluctuations caused by production capacity, demand, or seasonality. Fluctuations in inventory levels can affect costs and profits, leading to misleading results.

 

Therefore Correct answer is (D) A, B and D only.

Question 8
Marginal-costing technique is useful for: (UGC NET Commerce Jun 2014)
  • A.Make or Buy decisions
  • B.Profit planning
  • C.Shut-down decisions
  • D.All of the above
Correct Answer: All of the above

Marginal costing is a costing technique that focuses on the variable costs of production and treats fixed costs as period costs that are expensed in full within the period they are incurred. This approach provides valuable insights for several decision-making scenarios:

 

1. Make or Buy Decisions: Marginal costing helps businesses evaluate whether to produce a product internally or purchase it from an external supplier by comparing the marginal costs of production with the purchase price.

 

2. Profit Planning: By analyzing the contribution margin (sales revenue minus variable costs), management can make informed decisions about pricing, cost control, and volume of sales necessary to achieve desired profit levels.

 

3. Shut-down Decisions: When faced with the option to continue or cease operations, marginal costing helps determine if the contribution margin from continuing operations exceeds the variable costs incurred, guiding management in making shut-down decisions.

Correct Answer: All of the above

The break-even point (BEP) is the level of sales at which total revenues equal total costs, resulting in neither profit nor loss. Several factors can affect the break-even point, including:

 

(A) Change in fixed costs: An increase in fixed costs (e.g., rent, salaries) will raise the break-even point, as more revenue is needed to cover these fixed expenses.

 

(B) Change in variable costs: If variable costs (e.g., costs of goods sold) increase, the contribution margin per unit decreases, requiring a higher sales volume to break even, thus raising the break-even point.

 

(C) Change in selling price: A decrease in the selling price reduces the contribution margin, which can also raise the break-even point. Conversely, an increase in the selling price will lower the break-even point.
